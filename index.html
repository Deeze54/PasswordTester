About these estimates

Every time shown assumes an attacker who already knows your password's length and character types, then guesses without stopping. Real attacks are often faster — they start with leaked-password databases and pattern rules, not blind brute force. That's what the warnings flag.

Passphrase note. When a passphrase is detected, the word-list time assumes a 10,000-word attacker dictionary. That's a middle-ground guess, not a precise figure — real word lists run from a few thousand to hundreds of thousands of words. Read it as a rough order of magnitude: more words, and rarer ones, push the true time much higher.

Avoid

  • Reusing a password anywhere — one leak exposes every account that shares it.
  • Names, birthdays, pets, sports teams — anything someone could look up about you gets tried first.
  • Simple swaps like a→@ or o→0. Cracking tools apply these rules automatically.
  • Single words, keyboard runs (qwerty, 12345), or a word with a number and symbol tacked on the end.
  • Typing a real password into sites you don't trust. This page runs fully offline, but most don't — assume anything you enter elsewhere can be logged.

What helps

  • Length beats complexity: a long, unique, random password from a password manager is strongest.
  • No manager? A passphrase of several uncommon, unrelated words is a memorable runner-up.
